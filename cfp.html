Overview and Scope

The AI for Meta-Science workshop concerns the use, evaluation, and governance of AI systems that shape the scientific process itself. Unlike AI-for-science venues that apply AI to advance discovery in the natural sciences, we treat the scientific enterprise itself — peer review, reproducibility and benchmarking, research evaluation, publication incentives, scientific integrity, and the design of funding and governance mechanisms — as the object of study and intervention.

We invite submissions to two tracks: a technical track for new methods, systems, and empirical analyses, and a position track for arguments about how the scientific ecosystem should change. Authors select a track at submission time; organizers may suggest moving a paper between tracks when it is a better fit.

Tracks and Topics

Technical Track

New methods, systems, benchmarks, datasets, and empirical analyses of AI in the scientific process. Topics include, but are not limited to:

  • AI/LLM-assisted peer review: methods, evaluation, deployments, and failure modes
  • Benchmarks and evaluation of AI scientists and autonomous research agents
  • Verification, reproducibility, and quality control of (AI-generated) scientific artifacts
  • Detection and analysis of AI-generated content and manipulation in scholarly communication
  • Robustness of review systems: hidden prompts, collusion, and gaming
  • Computational methods for reviewer assignment, calibration, and incentives
  • Science-of-science and bibliometric analyses of AI’s impact on research production and evaluation
  • Tools and infrastructure for scientific evaluation: checklists, claim verification, benchmarking platforms
  • Analyses of real-world deployments (e.g., conference-scale AI reviewing experiments, arXiv moderation)

Evaluated on soundness, novelty, and relevance to the workshop scope. Rigorous negative results and careful replications are welcome.

Position Track

Modeled on the ICML/NeurIPS position paper tracks: position papers argue for a viewpoint about what should be done, in contrast to technical papers, which report on advances that have already been accomplished. Submissions need not be technical, and we explicitly welcome perspectives from philosophy, science studies, law, and policy. Titles should begin with “Position:”. Topics include, but are not limited to:

  • What counts as a publication when AI scientists produce papers at scale?
  • Redesigning peer review, publication criteria, and incentive structures for the AI era
  • Credit, authorship, and career incentives for human and hybrid human–AI research
  • Governance, funding allocation, and institutional responses to AI-accelerated science
  • Ethics and epistemology of AI in the scientific process
  • Policies for AI use in reviewing and authoring at conferences, journals, and repositories

Evaluated primarily on the clarity of the position, the quality of argument and evidence, significance, and serious engagement with alternative views (not strawmen) — not on empirical novelty.

Submission Guidelines

  • Where: Submissions are made via OpenReview. The submission link will be posted here soon. If you are new to OpenReview, create your account well before the deadline — new-account moderation can take up to two weeks.
  • Length: Submissions are expected to be 4–8 pages, excluding references and appendices. Reviewers are not required to read beyond the main text.
  • Style: Use the NeurIPS 2026 LaTeX style file, with the footnote changed to “Submitted to AI for Meta-Science workshop (NeurIPS 2026)”. Submit a single PDF (appendices included in the same PDF). The NeurIPS paper checklist is not needed. Submissions that violate the formatting requirements may be desk-rejected.
  • Anonymization: Reviewing is double-blind; submissions must be fully anonymized, including links and supplementary material. Cite your own prior work in the third person.
  • AI use disclosure: In the spirit of the workshop, we ask authors to briefly disclose the role AI systems played in producing the submission (e.g., writing assistance, analysis, autonomous generation) — a short statement in the paper or submission form suffices.

Review Process

  • Double-blind review on OpenReview, with approximately three reviews per submission and no rebuttal phase.
  • The two tracks are reviewed with track-appropriate criteria (see above); the position track review pool includes interdisciplinary reviewers from meta-science, philosophy, and policy.
  • Organizers and PC members adhere to the NeurIPS conflict-of-interest policy; OpenReview’s COI tools are used throughout.

Archival Status and Dual Submission

  • This workshop is non-archival: accepted papers will be listed on the workshop website and OpenReview, but there are no formal proceedings. Posting to arXiv before or after the workshop is allowed and encouraged.
  • Work under review elsewhere (including the NeurIPS 2026 main conference) may be submitted, provided this complies with the other venue’s policy.
  • Work already accepted or published at an archival ML venue should not be submitted. For position papers, prior publication outside ML venues (e.g., in philosophy, policy, or domain journals) is acceptable.

Presentation and Attendance

  • All accepted papers are presented as posters; a subset will be selected for short contributed talks. Poster instructions will follow with acceptance notifications.
  • At least one author is expected to attend the workshop in person and present the work. Attendance requires NeurIPS 2026 workshop registration (separate from main-conference registration).
  • Camera-ready versions (de-anonymized, with an optional extra page) are due in November 2026; the exact date will be announced with notifications.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I submit work that is under review at NeurIPS, ICLR, or another venue?
Yes — concurrent submissions are welcome as long as the other venue’s policy allows it. If the paper is accepted at an archival venue before the workshop, please withdraw it from the workshop.
Will accepted papers appear in proceedings?
No. The workshop is non-archival: accepted papers are listed on this website and OpenReview only, so you retain full freedom to publish the work elsewhere later.
Is a position paper without experiments really in scope?
Yes — that is exactly what the position track is for. Position papers are judged on the strength and clarity of their argument, not on empirical results, and are reviewed with interdisciplinary criteria.
Can non-ML researchers (philosophy, science studies, policy, publishing) submit?
Absolutely. The workshop is sociotechnical by design, and the position track especially welcomes contributions from outside machine learning.
Do I need to attend in person?
At least one author is expected to attend in person and present. If exceptional circumstances prevent this, contact the organizers.
Can AI systems co-author submissions?
AI assistance is welcome — this is, after all, a workshop about AI in science. We ask for a brief disclosure of the role AI played in producing the submission. Responsibility for the content rests with the human authors.
