About the Workshop

AI is rapidly transforming how scientific work is produced. Beyond accelerating computational workflows, AI systems increasingly support or automate major parts of research: hypothesis generation, experiment planning, analysis, writing, and even elements of wet-lab automation. As AI scientists and AI-augmented pipelines reduce the marginal cost of producing scientific artifacts, the scientific ecosystem faces a new bottleneck: evaluation, verification, and governance.

While the production side of science has scaled dramatically, quality control — peer review, reproducibility norms, publication criteria, incentive structures, and funding allocation — is lagging behind. Record submission numbers at major AI conferences and a growing reviewer crisis make this gap visible year after year. This raises pressing questions for both academia and industry: What counts as a publication when AI scientists can produce research papers at scale? How do we value and incentivize scientists and promote their careers? How can we maintain quality control in science?

This workshop aims to establish AI for meta-science as a distinct research area at the intersection of machine learning, science policy, and the study of science. We bring together researchers from meta-science, AI, peer review, and evaluation science with conference organizers, journal editors, and arXiv moderators to discuss and develop solutions — and to build the future of the scientific enterprise together.

The program combines invited talks, contributed talks, a poster session, a panel discussion, and unconference-style breakout sessions where attendees propose their own topics for collaborative research sprints and community building. The workshop aims to produce a position paper with a set of recommendations based on participant input, and to launch a working group on AI for meta-science.

Call for Papers

We welcome contributions on the use, evaluation, and governance of AI systems that shape the scientific process itself. Note that, unlike AI-for-science venues that apply AI to advance discovery in the natural sciences, this workshop treats the scientific enterprise itself as the object of study and intervention. Submissions are invited to one of two tracks.

Technical Track

New methods, systems, benchmarks, and empirical analyses of AI in the scientific process. Topics include, but are not limited to:

  • AI/LLM-assisted peer review: methods, evaluation, deployments, and failure modes
  • Benchmarks and evaluation of AI scientists and autonomous research agents
  • Verification, reproducibility, and quality control of (AI-generated) scientific artifacts
  • Detection and analysis of AI-generated content and manipulation in scholarly communication
  • Robustness of review systems: hidden prompts, collusion, and gaming
  • Computational methods for reviewer assignment, calibration, and incentives
  • Science-of-science analyses of AI’s impact on research production and evaluation
  • Tools and infrastructure for scientific evaluation: checklists, claim verification, benchmarking platforms

Position Track

Modeled on the ICML/NeurIPS position paper tracks: position papers argue for a viewpoint about what should be done, in contrast to technical papers, which report on advances that have already been accomplished. Submissions need not be technical. Titles should begin with “Position:”. Topics include, but are not limited to:

  • What counts as a publication when AI scientists produce papers at scale?
  • Redesigning peer review, publication criteria, and incentive structures for the AI era
  • Credit, authorship, and career incentives for human and hybrid human–AI research
  • Governance, funding allocation, and institutional responses to AI-accelerated science
  • Ethics and epistemology of AI in the scientific process
  • Policies for AI use in reviewing and authoring at conferences, journals, and repositories

Position papers are judged primarily on the clarity of the position, the quality of the argument and evidence, and serious engagement with alternative views — not on empirical novelty.

Submission Guidelines

  • Format: Submissions may be short papers (up to 4 pages) or full-length papers (up to 9 pages), excluding references and appendices. Reviewers are not required to read beyond the main text.
  • Style: Use the NeurIPS 2026 LaTeX style file. Submissions must be fully anonymized for double-blind review; cite your own prior work in the third person. Formatting violations may lead to desk rejection.
  • Review process: Double-blind, via OpenReview, with approximately three reviews per submission. The submission link will be posted here soon.
  • Non-archival: The workshop is non-archival — accepted papers will be listed on the workshop site and OpenReview, but there will be no formal proceedings. Submissions under review at other venues (including the NeurIPS 2026 main conference) are welcome; papers accepted at an archival ML venue before the workshop should be withdrawn. For position papers, prior publication outside ML venues is acceptable.
  • Presentation: All accepted papers are presented as posters; a subset will be selected for contributed talks. At least one author is expected to attend the workshop in person (NeurIPS workshop registration required).
  • AI use disclosure: In the spirit of the workshop, we ask authors to briefly disclose the role AI systems played in producing the submission (e.g., writing assistance, analysis, autonomous generation).
